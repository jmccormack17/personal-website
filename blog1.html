Data Science, Machine Learning, and Artificial Intelligence: What Are They?

Data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence are some of the most popular buzzwords in science and technology today. While these concepts are not new, their application and implementation across industries have ramped up significantly in recent years. Although these terms are interrelated and sometimes used interchangeably, they are distinct, and understanding the differences between them is essential for fully comprehending their scope and use.

Data Science can be described as the science of extracting meaningful insights from data. While this definition may sound straightforward, in practice, it is not. The process by which a data scientist or someone utilizing data science tools extracts insights depends on the context, including the specific problem, the industry, and the data characteristics. Data science is not a fixed methodology for uncovering meaning within data, but rather an evolving process that transforms raw data into information by whatever means necessary. Data science often draws from fields such as statistics, computer science, and the specific domain where the data resides.

Machine Learning involves creating statistical algorithms that allow systems to “learn” from data and make decisions. The key component of machine learning is that the program (often called the model) doesn’t make decisions based on information it was programmed with, but instead with information it learned. What makes machine learning particularly effective is the ability of many algorithms to be applied across different domains. For example, a machine learning algorithm designed to predict disease outbreaks could also be used to make stock trading decisions, detect spam emails, or predict the outcome of a football game.

Artificial Intelligence is the broadest and most talked about field. AI refers to any machine or system that imitates human intelligence. If this definition sounds vague, that’s because it is. There is debate among researchers, scientists, and the public over what’s considered artificial intelligence. Even the words in the definition itself such as “system,” “imitate,” “human,” and “intelligence” can be interpreted subjectively. Without going into epistemology to define what is or isn’t artificial intelligence, the practical approach is to use the consensus of what is recognized as AI and base understanding of the term on that.

To better understand the three fields and their relationship, consider some everyday examples. An online book retailer may want to gather insights into the spending habits of their customers. They might investigate statistics on how frequently club members purchase books compared to non-members. They may also want to know which books are most popular at certain times of the year, and which types of customers are buying them. These insights can be uncovered using data science. But what if the company wants to predict how much revenue it will make in the next quarter? They could use a machine learning algorithm to predict future revenue by “learning” from the data of previous quarters, identifying patterns like the impact of new book releases, holidays, and customer spending trends. By forecasting how much revenue will be generated next quarter, the company can spend their current budget accordingly. Finally, the company might incorporate an artificial intelligence chatbot to enhance customer experience. The chatbot doesn’t learn from interactions, but the text it generates appears human-like. It uses pre-recorded responses to assist customers with common questions, such as tracking their books or finding recommendations.

These technologies, data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence are being implemented across various industries, and their usage shows no sign of slowing down. While being an expert in these areas is not necessary, having a conceptual understanding of how these systems work is essential for staying informed and making educated decisions in a tech-driven world.
